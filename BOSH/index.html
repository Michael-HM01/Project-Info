Introduction to BOSH

Basic Occupational Safety and Health (BOSH) is a cornerstone of workplace safety programs. It provides essential knowledge for both employers and employees, ensuring they understand how to recognize hazards, prevent accidents, and maintain a healthy work environment. BOSH emphasizes prevention rather than reaction, aiming to reduce risks before they cause harm.

A strong safety culture not only protects workers but also increases productivity, reduces downtime, and improves morale across the organization.

Republic Act No. 11058 (Occupational Safety and Health Law)

Also known as the OSH Law of 2018, Republic Act No. 11058 strengthens the implementation of workplace safety and health standards in the Philippines. It ensures that all workers are protected from occupational risks and hazards by requiring employers to:

  • Provide a safe and healthy workplace free from known dangers.
  • Conduct safety orientations and training for all employees.
  • Supply proper personal protective equipment (PPE) and emergency facilities.
  • Develop and implement a written safety and health program approved by DOLE.
  • Report workplace accidents, illnesses, and hazards promptly.

The law also gives workers the right to refuse unsafe work and imposes penalties on employers who fail to comply with OSH standards. It is enforced by the Department of Labor and Employment (DOLE) through regular inspections and compliance monitoring.

Basic Information

Workers have the right to a safe, healthful workplace as mandated by the OSH Law (RA 11058). These include:

  • Right to know workplace hazards and preventive measures.
  • Right to safety training and protective equipment.
  • Right to refuse unsafe work.
  • Right to medical care and compensation for work-related injuries.
  • Physical: noise, heat, poor lighting.
  • Chemical: exposure to toxic substances.
  • Ergonomic: poor posture, repetitive tasks.
  • Biological: viruses, bacteria, fungi.
  • Psychosocial: stress, bullying, fatigue.

Effective emergency procedures save lives by reducing panic and ensuring proper response.

  • Know escape routes and assembly points.
  • Use PPE when responding to hazardous situations.
  • Participate in regular drills and training.

Good Housekeeping

Good housekeeping is a fundamental part of BOSH. It ensures that every workplace remains organized, clean, and free from unnecessary hazards. Proper housekeeping not only prevents accidents like slips, trips, and fires‚Äîit also improves morale, productivity, and the overall safety culture within an organization.

  • Maintain cleanliness and order in all work areas.
  • Keep tools and materials properly stored when not in use.
  • Dispose of waste safely and promptly.
  • Mark and clear emergency exits and passageways.
  • Inspect work areas regularly to identify and eliminate hazards.

Within the BOSH framework, good housekeeping supports the concept of continuous improvement, aligning closely with Japanese management systems like Gemba, 5S, and Kaizen.

Gemba is a Japanese term meaning "the real place"‚Äîthe actual location where work happens. In the context of BOSH, this encourages managers and safety officers to visit the work area to observe how tasks are performed, identify risks, and implement improvements directly at the source.

  • Observe actual working conditions to detect unsafe practices.
  • Communicate directly with workers for feedback and solutions.
  • Promote hands-on leadership and immediate corrective action.

Kaizen means ‚Äúchange for the better.‚Äù In workplace safety, Kaizen focuses on small, continuous improvements that make the environment safer and more efficient.

  • Encourage employees to identify and suggest safety improvements.
  • Regularly review safety performance and make incremental changes.
  • Promote teamwork and accountability in maintaining safety standards.

Through Kaizen, BOSH principles are sustained and integrated into everyday work, ensuring that safety evolves alongside productivity.

The 5S system is a workplace organization method that improves safety, efficiency, and quality. It aligns perfectly with BOSH principles because it minimizes hazards through order and discipline.

  • Seiri (Sort): Remove unnecessary items from the workplace.
  • Seiton (Set in order): Arrange tools and materials for easy access.
  • Seiso (Shine): Clean the work area regularly.
  • Seiketsu (Standardize): Create visual standards for organization and cleanliness.
  • Shitsuke (Sustain): Maintain discipline and continuous improvement.

Machine and Equipment Safety

Machine safety is a vital component of occupational safety and health. It ensures that equipment is properly maintained, used, and safeguarded to protect workers from mechanical hazards. In accordance with Philippine OSH Standards (Rule 1202), all machinery should be designed, operated, and maintained to minimize risks.

Safe handling includes training workers in proper use, storage, and maintenance of machines. Operators must understand operational limits, emergency stops, and lockout/tagout procedures.

  • Only trained personnel should operate machinery.
  • Ensure all safety guards are in place before operation.
  • Perform pre-operation checks regularly.

Rule 1202 of the Philippine Occupational Safety and Health Standards defines the safety requirements for mechanical equipment and machinery. It mandates that all machines must have sufficient protection to prevent injury, and that operators must receive regular safety instruction.

  • All moving parts must be securely guarded.
  • Emergency stop controls should be clearly accessible.
  • Periodic inspection and maintenance are mandatory.

Wearing proper clothing and PPE is crucial when operating or working near machines. Avoid loose clothing, jewelry, or long hair that can be caught in moving parts.

  • Wear safety goggles, gloves, and non-slip footwear.
  • Tie back long hair and avoid dangling accessories.

Quality control ensures that machinery operates safely and efficiently. Regular calibration, inspection, and testing prevent malfunction and accidents.

  • Perform periodic machine calibration and safety audits.
  • Document all inspections and repairs.

Common mechanical hazards include rotating parts, shear points, pinch points, and flying debris. These hazards can cause severe injuries if safeguards are absent.

  • Ensure all belts, pulleys, and gears have guards.
  • Train operators to recognize mechanical risks.

Safeguards protect operators and other personnel from injuries caused by machine operations. They must be durable, reliable, and not interfere with machine function.

  • Guards: Physical barriers that prevent access to dangerous areas.
  • Devices: Sensors or mechanisms that stop operations when hazards are detected.
  • Safety Controls: Emergency stops and interlocks to prevent unintended start-up.
  • Gates: Moveable barriers providing controlled access.
  • Robots: Automated systems designed to reduce human exposure to hazards.
  • Location & Distance: Placing dangerous equipment away from frequent worker access zones.
  • Feeding & Ejection Methods: Automatic or semi-automatic systems to minimize human contact.
  • Miscellaneous Aids: Shields, holding tools, or push sticks used for added safety.

Occupational Health

Occupational Health focuses on safeguarding the physical, mental, and social well-being of all workers. It‚Äôs not just about avoiding illness‚Äîit‚Äôs about creating a balanced environment where employees thrive, productivity increases, and risks are controlled through awareness and proactive action.

Safety is the condition of being protected from harm. Occupational Safety ensures workplaces are free from risks that may cause injury or illness. It includes identifying hazards, implementing controls, and training employees to maintain a safety-first mindset.

  • Regular risk assessments and hazard mapping
  • Employee safety training and PPE compliance
  • Clear emergency procedures and signage
  • Monitoring and reporting of unsafe conditions

Occupational exposure occurs when workers come into contact with harmful physical, chemical, or biological agents during work. These exposures may be brief or prolonged, leading to health risks such as poisoning, infections, or long-term diseases.

  • Identify and measure exposure levels in the workplace.
  • Limit exposure duration through administrative control.
  • Use protective barriers, ventilation, and PPE.

Work-related diseases develop due to poor working conditions, unsafe environments, or repetitive strain. These diseases can cause chronic pain, disability, or even death if preventive measures are ignored.

  • Respiratory illnesses (asthma, silicosis, asbestosis)
  • Hearing loss from prolonged exposure to noise
  • Skin diseases from irritants or allergens
  • Musculoskeletal disorders from repetitive motions
  • Mental health concerns caused by stress or fatigue

Safety Data Sheets (SDS) provide detailed information about hazardous substances, including handling, storage, and emergency measures. They are essential for ensuring workplace safety and compliance with chemical safety regulations.

A Safety Data Sheet (SDS) provides key safety information for chemicals used in the workplace. It details a chemical‚Äôs properties, hazards, protective measures, and emergency response instructions.

Importance of SDS:

  • Informs workers about potential health effects and safe handling.
  • Guides first-aid and spill response during emergencies.
  • Ensures compliance with OSH and DOLE regulations.
  • Helps employers maintain a safe and informed workplace.

Handle hazardous chemicals with proper PPE and ensure containers are properly labeled.

Hazardous chemicals are substances that pose health or physical risks to workers. Exposure can occur through various routes and can lead to acute or chronic health conditions.

Major Entry Points in the Body:

  • Inhalation (gases, dusts, vapors)
  • Skin absorption (liquid contact)
  • Ingestion (contaminated food or hands)
  • Injection (needlesticks or punctures)
  • Eye contact (splashes or airborne particles)

5 Ways to Identify Hazardous Chemicals:

  • Check warning labels and hazard symbols.
  • Review Safety Data Sheets (SDS).
  • Refer to the manufacturer‚Äôs hazard classification.
  • Observe strong odors or fumes (caution advised).
  • Consult the company‚Äôs chemical inventory list.

Ways to Control Chemical Hazards:

  • Substitution: Replace toxic materials with safer ones.
  • Engineering Controls: Install exhaust or ventilation systems.
  • Administrative Controls: Rotate tasks to limit exposure.
  • PPE: Use gloves, respirators, and protective eyewear.
  • Emergency Preparedness: Proper labeling and spill kits.
